Power Series

Here is probably the most important thing you learn in this class:

Definition

Let F(x)=∑n=0∞an(x−c)n\displaystyle F\p{x} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n\p{x - c}^nF(x)=n=0∑∞​an​(x−c)n. FFF is called a power series and we call ccc the center of FFF.

Power series are great because they have a bunch of nice properties:

Proposition (properties of power series)

Let F(x)=∑n=0∞an(x−c)n\displaystyle F\p{x} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n\p{x - c}^nF(x)=n=0∑∞​an​(x−c)n be a power series.

  1. There exists a number R∈[0,∞]R \in \br{0, \infty}R∈[0,∞] called the radius of convergence of FFF such that if ∣x−c∣<R\abs{x - c} < R∣x−c∣<R, then FFF converges absolutely and if ∣x−c∣>R\abs{x - c} > R∣x−c∣>R, then FFF diverges. If R=0R = 0R=0, then the series only converges at its center and if R=∞R = \inftyR=∞, then FFF converges absolutely everywhere.

  2. The coefficients can be expressed in terms of the derivatives of FFF:

    an=F(n)(c)n!a_n = \frac{F^{\p{n}}\p{c}}{n!}an​=n!F(n)(c)​

  3. You can differentiate term-by-term, i.e., you can pretend the sum isn't there and take the derivative like normal:

    F′(x)=∑n=1∞nan(x−c)n−1F'\p{x} = \sum_{n=1}^\infty na_n\p{x - c}^{n-1}F′(x)=n=1∑∞​nan​(x−c)n−1

    F′F'F′ is also a power series and it has the same radius as FFF.

  4. You can also integrate term-by-term:

    ∫F(x) dx=C+∑n=0∞ann+1(x−c)n+1\int F\p{x} \,\diff{x} = C + \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{a_n}{n+1} \p{x - c}^{n+1}∫F(x)dx=C+n=0∑∞​n+1an​​(x−c)n+1

    Like F′F'F′, the antiderivative of FFF is a power series with the same radius of convergence as FFF.

Taylor Polynomials

If you look in the previous section, given a power series FFF, we know its derivatives right away from the coefficients. So, it's natural to wonder what happens if you go the other way, which is what Taylor series are all about.

Definition

Let fff be a function with nnn derivatives. Then the nnn-th Taylor polynomial centered at x=cx = cx=c for fff is

Tn(x)=f(c)+f′(c)(x−c)+f′′(c)2(x−c)2+⋯+f(n)(c)n!(x−c)n=∑k=0nf(k)(c)k!(x−c)k.\begin{aligned} T_n\p{x} &= f\p{c} + f'\p{c}\p{x - c} + \frac{f''\p{c}}{2}\p{x - c}^2 + \cdots + \frac{f^{\p{n}}\p{c}}{n!}\p{x - c}^n \\ &= \sum_{k=0}^n \frac{f^{\p{k}}\p{c}}{k!}\p{x - c}^k. \end{aligned}Tn​(x)​=f(c)+f′(c)(x−c)+2f′′(c)​(x−c)2+⋯+n!f(n)(c)​(x−c)n=k=0∑n​k!f(k)(c)​(x−c)k.​

If c=0c = 0c=0, then the Taylor polynomial is also called the Maclaurin polynomial.

If n=1n = 1n=1, then you get T1(x)=f(c)+f′(c)(x−c)T_1\p{x} = f\p{c} + f'\p{c}\p{x - c}T1​(x)=f(c)+f′(c)(x−c), which is the tangent line to fff at x=cx = cx=c, which is the linear function that best approximates fff near x=cx = cx=c. Building off of that, we can interpret Tn(x)T_n\p{x}Tn​(x) as the nnn-th degree polynomial that best approximates fff near x=cx = cx=c.

With this interpretation, a natural question is how good is this approximation? This is answered with the following error bound:

Theorem

Suppose f(n+1)f^{\p{n+1}}f(n+1) exists and is continuous. Then if ∣f(n+1)(u)∣≤K\abs{f^{\p{n+1}}\p{u}} \leq K∣∣​f(n+1)(u)∣∣​≤K for all uuu between ccc and xxx, then

∣Tn(x)−f(x)∣≤K(n+1)!∣x−c∣n+1.\abs{T_n\p{x} - f\p{x}} \leq \frac{K}{\p{n + 1}!}\abs{x - c}^{n+1}.∣Tn​(x)−f(x)∣≤(n+1)!K​∣x−c∣n+1.

Notice that KKK depends on xxx, i.e., if you change xxx, you have to recalculate KKK. Also notice that if xxx is really far away from ccc, then ∣x−c∣n+1\abs{x - c}^{n+1}∣x−c∣n+1 becomes very large, and if xxx is really close to ccc, then ∣x−c∣n+1\abs{x - c}^{n+1}∣x−c∣n+1 becomes very small, which aligns with the interpretation that Tn(x)T_n\p{x}Tn​(x) best approximates fff near x=cx = cx=c.

Here's a helpful mnemonic to help you remember the error bound formula: if you have Tn(x)T_n\p{x}Tn​(x), then the "next term" in the polynomial would look like

f(n+1)(c)(n+1)!(x−c)n+1.\frac{f^{\p{n+1}}\p{c}}{\p{n + 1}!}\p{x - c}^{n+1}.(n+1)!f(n+1)(c)​(x−c)n+1.

Put everything in absolute values and replace ∣f(n+1)(c)∣\abs{f^{\p{n+1}}\p{c}}∣∣​f(n+1)(c)∣∣​ with the upper bound KKK, and that gives you

∣f(n+1)(c)∣(n+1)!∣x−c∣n+1⇝K(n+1)!∣x−c∣n+1.\frac{\abs{f^{\p{n+1}}\p{c}}}{\p{n + 1}!}\abs{x - c}^{n+1} \rightsquigarrow \frac{K}{\p{n + 1}!}\abs{x - c}^{n+1}.(n+1)!∣∣​f(n+1)(c)∣∣​​∣x−c∣n+1⇝(n+1)!K​∣x−c∣n+1.

Taylor Series

Taylor polynomials already look like power series, except Taylor polynomials are finite sums. Once you turn it into an infinite sum, you get a Taylor series:

Definition

Let fff be an infinitely differentiable function. Then the Taylor series of fff at x=cx = cx=c is

T(x)=∑n=0∞f(n)(c)n!(x−c)n.T\p{x} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{f^{\p{n}}\p{c}}{n!}\p{x - c}^n.T(x)=n=0∑∞​n!f(n)(c)​(x−c)n.

If c=0c = 0c=0, then T(x)T\p{x}T(x) is also called the Maclaurin series of fff.

You would hope that because Taylor polynomials are good approximations of fff that Taylor series should be an "infinitely good" approximation of fff, i.e., the Taylor series of fff converges to fff. Unfortunately, that isn't always the case.
