Problem 1

Let X1,…,XnX_1, \ldots, X_nX1​,…,Xn​ be a random sample from a uniform distribution on the interval (θ−1,θ+1)\p{\theta-1, \theta+1}(θ−1,θ+1), where −∞<θ<∞-\infty < \theta < \infty−∞<θ<∞.

  1. Find the method of moment estimator for θ\thetaθ.
  2. Is your estimator in part 1 an unbiased estimator of θ\thetaθ? Justify your answer.
  3. Given the following n=5n = 5n=5 observations of XXX, give a point estimate of θ\thetaθ.

Solution.

  1. The mean of the uniform distribution is

    E[Xi]=θ,\E\br{X_i} = \theta,E[Xi​]=θ,

    so the method of moment estimate for θ\thetaθ is given by x‾=θ^\mean{x} = \widehat{\theta}x=θ. Thus, the estimator is

    θ^=X‾.\boxed{\widehat{\theta} = \mean{X}}.θ=X​.

  2. Yes. By linearity of expectation,

    E[θ^]=E[1n∑i=1nXi]=1n∑i=1nE[Xi]=θ.\E\br{\widehat{\theta}} = \E\br{\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \E\br{X_i} = \theta.E[θ]=E[n1​i=1∑n​Xi​]=n1​i=1∑n​E[Xi​]=θ.

  3. We have

    θ^=x‾=7.382.\boxed{ \widehat{\theta} = \mean{x} = 7.382 }.θ=x=7.382​.

Assume that the data was pulled from N(μ,σ2)\mathcal{N}\p{\mu, \sigma^2}N(μ,σ2). Since the population standard deviation is unknown, we need to use a ttt-distribution. Our test statistic is

T=X‾−μS/n∼t(n−1).T = \frac{\mean{X} - \mu}{S/\sqrt{n}} \sim t\p{n-1}.T=S/n​X−μ​∼t(n−1).

x‾±tα2(n−1)sn,\mean{x} \pm t_{\frac{\alpha}{2}}\p{n-1} \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}},x±t2α​​(n−1)n​s​,

Problem 3

Let x1,…,xnx_1, \ldots, x_nx1​,…,xn​ be an observed sample drawn independently from an exponential distribution, Exp⁡(λ)\operatorname{Exp}\p{\lambda}Exp(λ). Assume that the prior on λ\lambdaλ is characterized by a Gamma distribution, Γ(α,β)\Gamma\p{\alpha, \beta}Γ(α,β), with pdf

π(λ)=βαΓ(α)λα−1e−βλ,0<x<∞.\pi\p{\lambda} = \frac{\beta^\alpha}{\Gamma\p{\alpha}} \lambda^{\alpha-1} e^{-\beta\lambda}, \quad 0 < x < \infty.π(λ)=Γ(α)βα​λα−1e−βλ,0<x<∞.

Note that the pdf of an exponential distribution is given by

f(x)=λe−λx,0<x<∞.f\p{x} = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, \quad 0 < x < \infty.f(x)=λe−λx,0<x<∞.
  1. Find the posterior distribution for λ\lambdaλ, π(λ | x)\pi\p{\lambda \given x}π(λ∣x).
  2. Calculate the mean of the posterior distribution.
Solution.

  1. The starting point for these types of problems is

    posterior∝likelihood×prior.\mathrm{posterior} \propto \mathrm{likelihood} \times \mathrm{prior}.posterior∝likelihood×prior.

    Here, ∝\propto∝ means that the two things only differ by a constant factor. Here, constant means anything that doesn't depend on λ\lambdaλ. We don't lose any information about the distribution when dropping factors without λ\lambdaλ in them because we can always figure out the normalization constant by integrating with respect to λ\lambdaλ. Actually, we don't even need the normalization constant to figure out the distribution.

    The likelihood is given by

    f(x1,…,xn∣λ)=∏i=1nλe−λxi=λne−λnx‾. f\p{x_1, \ldots, x_n \mid \lambda} = \prod_{i=1}^n \lambda e^{-\lambda x_i} = \lambda^n e^{-\lambda n\mean{x}}.f(x1​,…,xn​∣λ)=i=1∏n​λe−λxi​=λne−λnx.

    The prior is

    π(λ)=βαΓ(α)λα−1e−βλ∝λα−1e−βλ. \pi\p{\lambda} = \frac{\beta^\alpha}{\Gamma\p{\alpha}} \lambda^{\alpha-1} e^{-\beta\lambda} \propto \lambda^{\alpha-1} e^{-\beta\lambda}.π(λ)=Γ(α)βα​λα−1e−βλ∝λα−1e−βλ.

    Putting everything together,

    π(λ | x)∝λne−λnx‾λα−1e−βλ∝λα+n−1e−λ(β+nx‾).\begin{aligned} \pi\p{\lambda \given x} &\propto \lambda^n e^{-\lambda n\mean{x}}\lambda^{\alpha-1} e^{-\beta\lambda} \\ &\propto \lambda^{\alpha+n-1} e^{-\lambda\p{\beta+n\mean{x}}}. \end{aligned}π(λ∣x)​∝λne−λnxλα−1e−βλ∝λα+n−1e−λ(β+nx).​

    By comparing to our known distributions, we see that the posterior distribution is

    Γ(α+n,β+nx‾).\boxed{\Gamma\p{\alpha + n, \beta + n\mean{x}}}.Γ(α+n,β+nx)​.

  2. The mean of a Γ(α,β)\Gamma\p{\alpha, \beta}Γ(α,β) distribution (with the notation in the problem) is αβ\frac{\alpha}{\beta}βα​, so the mean of the posterior distribution is

    α+nβ+nx‾.\boxed{\frac{\alpha + n}{\beta + n\mean{x}}}.β+nxα+n​​.

Problem 5

  1. Let X1,…,XnX_1, \ldots, X_nX1​,…,Xn​ be a random sample from a continuous random variable with pdf
f(x,θ)=θxθ−1,0<x<1,0<θ.f\p{x, \theta} = \theta x^{\theta-1}, \quad 0 < x < 1, \quad 0 < \theta.f(x,θ)=θxθ−1,0<x<1,0<θ.
  1. Find a sufficient statistic YYY for θ\thetaθ.
  2. Show that the maximum likelihood estimator θ^\widehat{\theta}θ is a function of YYY.
  3. Argue that θ^\widehat{\theta}θ is also a sufficient statistic for θ\thetaθ.
Solution.

  1. To find a sufficient statistic, we can factor the likelihood function and eyeball it.

    f(x1,…,xn,θ)=∏i=1nθxiθ−1=θn(∏i=1nxi)θ−1.f\p{x_1, \ldots, x_n, \theta} = \prod_{i=1}^n \theta x_i^{\theta-1} = \theta^n \p{\prod_{i=1}^n x_i}^{\theta-1}.f(x1​,…,xn​,θ)=i=1∏n​θxiθ−1​=θn(i=1∏n​xi​)θ−1.

    So a sufficient statistic is Y=∏i=1nXiY = \prod_{i=1}^n X_iY=∏i=1n​Xi​. We can write down the factorization explicitly:

    φ(y,θ)=θnyθ−1,h(x1,…,xn)=1.\varphi\p{y, \theta} = \theta^n y^{\theta-1}, \quad h\p{x_1, \ldots, x_n} = 1.φ(y,θ)=θnyθ−1,h(x1​,…,xn​)=1.

  2. From our computation above, the log-likelihood function is

    ln⁡L(θ)=nln⁡θ+(θ−1)ln⁡∏i=1nxi.\ln L\p{\theta} = n\ln \theta + \p{\theta-1} \ln \prod_{i=1}^n x_i.lnL(θ)=nlnθ+(θ−1)lni=1∏n​xi​.

    Taking derivatives and solving for θ\thetaθ gives the MLE

    θ^=−nln⁡∏i=1nXi=−nln⁡Y.\widehat{\theta} = -\frac{n}{\ln \prod_{i=1}^n X_i} = -\frac{n}{\ln Y}.θ=−ln∏i=1n​Xi​n​=−lnYn​.

  3. We can solve for YYY in terms of θ^\widehat{\theta}θ:

    Y=exp⁡(−nθ^),Y = \exp\p{-\frac{n}{\widehat{\theta}}},Y=exp(−θn​),

    so plugging this into our factorization,

    f(x1,…,xn,θ)=φ(exp⁡(−nθ^),θ)h(x1,…,xn).f\p{x_1, \ldots, x_n, \theta} = \varphi\p{\exp\p{-\frac{n}{\widehat{\theta}}}, \theta} h\p{x_1, \ldots, x_n}.f(x1​,…,xn​,θ)=φ(exp(−θn​),θ)h(x1​,…,xn​).

    This shows that θ^\widehat{\theta}θ is a sufficient statistic for θ\thetaθ.
