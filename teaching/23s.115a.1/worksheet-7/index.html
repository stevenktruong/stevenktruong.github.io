Problem 1

Let VVV be a finite dimensional vector space over R\RR. Show that if dim⁡V\dim VdimV is odd, then every T∈L(V)T \in \mathcal{L}\p{V}T∈L(V) has an eigenvalue.

Solution.

TTT is not assumed to be a matrix, so we can't jump straight into characteristic polynomials. Instead, we need to start with a matrix representation of TTT.

Let β\betaβ be a basis of VVV and consider the matrix A=[T]βA = \br{T}_\betaA=[T]β​. Since the underlying field is R\RR, we can use calculus. The characteristic polynomial pA ⁣:R→R\func{p_A}{\R}{\R}pA​:R→R is a continuous function, so we may invoke the intermediate value theorem. Since pAp_ApA​ is an odd degree polynomial, there are two cases:

{lim⁡λ→∞pA(λ)=∞,lim⁡λ→−∞pA(λ)=−∞or{lim⁡λ→∞pA(λ)=−∞,lim⁡λ→−∞pA(λ)=∞.\begin{cases} \displaystyle\lim_{\lambda\to\infty} p_A\p{\lambda} = \infty, \\[2ex] \displaystyle\lim_{\lambda\to-\infty} p_A\p{\lambda} = -\infty \end{cases} \quad\text{or}\quad \begin{cases} \displaystyle\lim_{\lambda\to\infty} p_A\p{\lambda} = -\infty, \\[2ex] \displaystyle\lim_{\lambda\to-\infty} p_A\p{\lambda} = \infty. \end{cases}⎩⎨⎧​λ→∞lim​pA​(λ)=∞,λ→−∞lim​pA​(λ)=−∞​or⎩⎨⎧​λ→∞lim​pA​(λ)=−∞,λ→−∞lim​pA​(λ)=∞.​

In either case, the intermediate value theorem tells us that pAp_ApA​ is surjective. In particular, there exists λ∈R\lambda \in \Rλ∈R such that pA(λ)=0p_A\p{\lambda} = 0pA​(λ)=0, i.e., λ\lambdaλ is an eigenvalue of AAA, so there exists a non-zero vector [v]β\br{v}_\beta[v]β​ such that A[v]β=λ[v]βA\br{v}_\beta = \lambda \br{v}_\betaA[v]β​=λ[v]β​. But this means

[Tv]β=A[v]β=λ[v]β=[λv]β  ⟺  Tv=λv.\br{Tv}_\beta = A\br{v}_\beta = \lambda\br{v}_\beta = \br{\lambda v}_\beta \iff Tv = \lambda v.[Tv]β​=A[v]β​=λ[v]β​=[λv]β​⟺Tv=λv.

Thus, because v≠0v \neq 0v=0, it follows that λ\lambdaλ is an eigenvalue of TTT.

Problem 6

Let VVV be a vector space over F\FF of dimension nnn, let T∈L(V)T \in \mathcal{L}\p{V}T∈L(V), let β\betaβ be an ordered basis of VVV. The determinant of TTT, denoted det⁡T\det TdetT is defined as det⁡T=det⁡[T]β\det T = \det \br{T}_\betadetT=det[T]β​.

  1. Prove that the determinant of TTT is independent of the choice of β\betaβ. Namely, prove that if β\betaβ and γ\gammaγ are two ordered bases of VVV, then det⁡[T]β=det⁡[T]γ\det \br{T}_\beta = \det \br{T}_\gammadet[T]β​=det[T]γ​.
  2. Prove that TTT is invertible if and only if det⁡T≠0\det T \neq 0detT=0.
  3. Prove that if TTT is invertible, then det⁡(T−1)=(det⁡T)−1\det\p{T^{-1}} = \p{\det T}^{-1}det(T−1)=(detT)−1.
  4. Prove that if S∈L(V)S \in \mathcal{L}\p{V}S∈L(V), then det⁡(TS)=det⁡(T)det⁡(S)\det\p{TS} = \det\p{T} \det\p{S}det(TS)=det(T)det(S).
  5. Prove that if λ∈F\lambda \in \Fλ∈F, then det⁡(T−λid⁡V)=det⁡([T]β−λIn)\det\p{T - \lambda \operatorname{id}_V} = \det\p{\br{T}_\beta - \lambda I_n}det(T−λidV​)=det([T]β​−λIn​).
Solution.

As before, all the facts we know about determinants only apply to (square) matrices. TTT does not have to be a matrix, but because det⁡T\det TdetT is defined using a matrix representation of TTT, these properties will follow by carefully applying the corresponding properties on the matrix [T]β\br{T}_\beta[T]β​.

  1. Recall that

    [T]β=[I]γβ[T]γ[I]βγ=([I]βγ)−1[T]γ[I]βγ.\br{T}_\beta = \br{I}_\gamma^\beta \br{T}_\gamma \br{I}_\beta^\gamma = \p{\br{I}_\beta^\gamma}^{-1} \br{T}_\gamma \br{I}_\beta^\gamma.[T]β​=[I]γβ​[T]γ​[I]βγ​=([I]βγ​)−1[T]γ​[I]βγ​.

    Thus, because the matrix determinant is multiplicative,

    det⁡[T]β=det⁡(([I]βγ)−1[T]γ[I]βγ)=det⁡([I]βγ)−1det⁡[T]γdet⁡[I]βγ=det⁡(([I]βγ)−1)det⁡([I]βγ)det⁡([T]γ)=det⁡(In)det⁡([T]γ)=det⁡[T]γ.\begin{aligned} \det \br{T}_\beta &= \det \p{\p{\br{I}_\beta^\gamma}^{-1} \br{T}_\gamma \br{I}_\beta^\gamma} \\ &= \det\p{\br{I}_\beta^\gamma}^{-1} \det\br{T}_\gamma \det\br{I}_\beta^\gamma \\ &= \det\p{\p{\br{I}_\beta^\gamma}^{-1}} \det\p{\br{I}_\beta^\gamma} \det\p{\br{T}_\gamma} \\ &= \det\p{I_n} \det\p{\br{T}_\gamma} \\ &= \det\br{T}_\gamma. \end{aligned}det[T]β​​=det(([I]βγ​)−1[T]γ​[I]βγ​)=det([I]βγ​)−1det[T]γ​det[I]βγ​=det(([I]βγ​)−1)det([I]βγ​)det([T]γ​)=det(In​)det([T]γ​)=det[T]γ​.​

  2. This follows from the fact that a matrix AAA is invertible if and only if det⁡A≠0\det A \neq 0detA=0. In the following, we apply this fact to A=[T]βA = \br{T}_\betaA=[T]β​.

    T is invertible  ⟺  [T]β is invertible  ⟺  det⁡[T]β≠0  ⟺  det⁡T≠0.\begin{aligned} T\text{ is invertible} &\iff \br{T}_\beta\text{ is invertible} \\ &\iff \det\br{T}_\beta \neq 0 \\ &\iff \det T \neq 0. \end{aligned}T is invertible​⟺[T]β​ is invertible⟺det[T]β​=0⟺detT=0.​

    The last equivalence holds since det⁡T=det⁡[T]β\det T = \det\br{T}_\betadetT=det[T]β​ by definition.

  3. From Part 2, we know det⁡T≠0\det T \neq 0detT=0, so (det⁡T)−1\p{\det T}^{-1}(detT)−1 is well-defined. Thus,

    1=det⁡In=det⁡[TT−1]β=det⁡([T]β[T−1]β)=det⁡([T]β)det⁡([T−1]β)=det⁡(T)det⁡(T−1)  ⟹  det⁡(T−1)=(det⁡T)−1.\begin{aligned} 1 &= \det I_n \\ &= \det \br{TT^{-1}}_\beta \\ &= \det \p{\br{T}_\beta \br{T^{-1}}_\beta} \\ &= \det\p{\br{T}_\beta} \det\p{\br{T^{-1}}_\beta} \\ &= \det\p{T} \det \p{T^{-1}} \\ \implies \det\p{T^{-1}} &= \p{\det T}^{-1}. \end{aligned}1⟹det(T−1)​=detIn​=det[TT−1]β​=det([T]β​[T−1]β​)=det([T]β​)det([T−1]β​)=det(T)det(T−1)=(detT)−1.​

    (Note that I can't say det⁡(TT−1)=det⁡(T)det⁡(T−1)\det\p{TT^{-1}} = \det\p{T} \det \p{T^{-1}}det(TT−1)=det(T)det(T−1) right away since we only know that the determinant is multiplicative for matrices. We prove this in the next part, though.)

  4. Recall that [TS]β=[T]β[S]β\br{TS}_\beta = \br{T}_\beta \br{S}_\beta[TS]β​=[T]β​[S]β​. Thus,

    det⁡(TS)=det⁡[TS]β=det⁡([T]β[S]β)=det⁡[T]βdet⁡[S]β=det⁡(T)det⁡(S).\begin{aligned} \det\p{TS} &= \det \br{TS}_\beta \\ &= \det\p{\br{T}_\beta \br{S}_\beta} \\ &= \det \br{T}_\beta \det \br{S}_\beta \\ &= \det\p{T} \det\p{S}. \end{aligned}det(TS)​=det[TS]β​=det([T]β​[S]β​)=det[T]β​det[S]β​=det(T)det(S).​

  5. This follows from the fact that [id⁡V]β=In\br{\operatorname{id}_V}_\beta = I_n[idV​]β​=In​, where n=dim⁡Vn = \dim Vn=dimV, and linearity of taking matrix representations:

    [T−λid⁡V]β=[T]β−λ[id⁡V]β=[T]β−λIn.\br{T - \lambda \operatorname{id}_V}_\beta = \br{T}_\beta - \lambda \br{\operatorname{id}_V}_\beta = \br{T}_\beta - \lambda I_n.[T−λidV​]β​=[T]β​−λ[idV​]β​=[T]β​−λIn​.

    Thus, by definition,

    det⁡(T−λid⁡V)=det⁡[T−λid⁡V]β=det⁡([T]β−λIn).\det\p{T - \lambda \operatorname{id}_V} = \det \br{T - \lambda \operatorname{id}_V}_\beta = \det\p{\br{T}_\beta - \lambda I_n}.det(T−λidV​)=det[T−λidV​]β​=det([T]β​−λIn​).
