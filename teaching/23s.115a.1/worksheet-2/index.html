Problem 4

Let VVV be a vector space, let u,v∈Vu, v \in Vu,v∈V be distinct. Show that {u,v}\set{u, v}{u,v} is linearly dependent if and only if uuu and vvv are multiples of each other.

Solution.

We have two directions to prove.

"  ⟹  \implies⟹"

Assume that {u,v}\set{u, v}{u,v} is linearly dependent. Then there exist a,b∈Fa, b \in \Fa,b∈F not both zero such that au+bv=0au + bv = 0au+bv=0. If a≠0a \neq 0a=0, then we may multiply both sides by a−1a^{-1}a−1 to get

u+bav=0  ⟹  u=(−ba)v.u + \frac{b}{a} v = 0 \implies u = \p{-\frac{b}{a}} v.u+ab​v=0⟹u=(−ab​)v.

Otherwise, b≠0b \neq 0b=0, and we analogously get

v=(−ab)u,v = \p{-\frac{a}{b}} u,v=(−ba​)u,

so in both cases, uuu and vvv are multiples of each.

"  ⟸  \impliedby⟸"

Suppose uuu and vvv are multiples of each other. Without loss of generality, we assume that uuu is a multiple of vvv, i.e., there exists a scalar c∈Fc \in \Fc∈F such that u=cvu = cvu=cv. In the other case, we can swap uuu and vvv in the following proof. Rearranging u=cvu = cvu=cv, we get

0=u−cv=1u+(−c)v.0 = u - cv = 1u + \p{-c}v.0=u−cv=1u+(−c)v.

Thus, the zero vector is a non-trivial linear combination of uuu and vvv since 1≠01 \neq 01=0, so {u,v}\set{u, v}{u,v} is linearly dependent.
