Limits of Functions

Definition

Given a function f ⁣:D→R\func{f}{D}{\R}f:D→R, we say that lim⁡x→af(x)=L\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a} f\p{x} = Lx→alim​f(x)=L if for every sequence {xn}n⊆D∖{a}\set{x_n}_n \subseteq D \setminus \set{a}{xn​}n​⊆D∖{a} such that xn→ax_n \to axn​→a, we have lim⁡n→∞f(xn)=L\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty} f\p{x_n} = Ln→∞lim​f(xn​)=L.

Compare with the definition of continuity:

f ⁣:D→R\func{f}{D}{\R}f:D→R is continuous at a∈Da \in Da∈D if for every sequence {xn}n⊆D\set{x_n}_n \subseteq D{xn​}n​⊆D such that xn→ax_n \to axn​→a, we have lim⁡n→∞f(xn)=L\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty} f\p{x_n} = Ln→∞lim​f(xn​)=L.

The difference here is that in continuity, the terms of the sequence are allowed to be aaa, but in the definition of a limit, the elements of your sequence are not allowed to be aaa. Beyond that, there's not much else to say. Everything is defined in terms of sequences, so as you would expect, all the usual limit laws apply.
