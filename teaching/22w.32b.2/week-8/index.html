Green's Theorem

Theorem

Let D\mathscr{D}D be a domain whose boundary, denoted ∂D\partial\mathscr{D}∂D, is a simple closed curve (i.e., a loop that doesn't intersect itself) oriented counter-clockwise. If F1F_1F1​ and F2F_2F2​ are continuously differentiable in an open set containing D\mathscr{D}D, then

∮∂DF1 dx+F2 dy=∬D∂F2∂x−∂F1∂y dA\oint_{\partial\mathscr{D}} F_1 \,\diff{x} + F_2 \,\diff{y} = \iint_{\mathscr{D}} \pderiv{F_2}{x} - \pderiv{F_1}{y} \,\diff{A}∮∂D​F1​dx+F2​dy=∬D​∂x∂F2​​−∂y∂F1​​dA

or equivalently,

∮∂DF⃗⋅dr⃗=∬Dcurl⁡zF⃗ dA.\oint_{\partial\mathscr{D}} \vec{F} \cdot \diff\vec{r} = \iint_{\mathscr{D}} \curl_z{\vec{F}} \,\diff{A}.∮∂D​F⋅dr=∬D​curlz​FdA.

Green's Theorem is a fundamental theorem of calculus. Compare it with the fundamental theorem of calculus in 1D:

∫abf′(x) dx=f(b)−f(a)\int_a^b f'\p{x} \,\diff{x} = f\p{b} - f\p{a}∫ab​f′(x)dx=f(b)−f(a)

This relates an integral of a derivative on the whole domain (f′f'f′ on the interval [a,b]\br{a, b}[a,b]) to an "integral" on the boundary of the function (fff at the points aaa and bbb).

Green's Theorem is a generalization of this: it relates an integral of a "derivative" on the whole domain (curl⁡zF⃗\curl_z{\vec{F}}curlz​F on D\mathscr{D}D) to an integral of the function on the boundary (F⃗\vec{F}F on ∂D\partial\mathscr{D}∂D). Every integral theorem we'll see in this class (i.e., Stokes' Theorem and the divergence theorem) will be in this form.

Assume that

Solution.

In our statement of Green's Theorem, we need ∂D\partial\mathscr{D}∂D to be a single simple closed curve, but in this problem, ∂D\partial\mathscr{D}∂D comprises of two such curves: ∂D=C1−C2\partial\mathscr{D} = \mathscr{C}_1 - \mathscr{C}_2∂D=C1​−C2​. So, we can't apply Green's Theorem right away, but we can use the following trick: divide D\mathscr{D}D like so:

We can apply Green's Theorem on both halves D1\mathscr{D}_1D1​ and D2\mathscr{D}_2D2​ of the domain to get

∬D1∂F2∂x−∂F1∂y dA=∮∂D1F⃗⋅dr⃗∬D2∂F2∂x−∂F1∂y dA=∮∂D2F⃗⋅dr⃗.\begin{aligned} \iint_{\mathscr{D}_1} \pderiv{F_2}{x} - \pderiv{F_1}{y} \,\diff{A} &= \oint_{\partial\mathscr{D}_1} \vec{F} \cdot \diff\vec{r} \\ \iint_{\mathscr{D}_2} \pderiv{F_2}{x} - \pderiv{F_1}{y} \,\diff{A} &= \oint_{\partial\mathscr{D}_2} \vec{F} \cdot \diff\vec{r}. \end{aligned}∬D1​​∂x∂F2​​−∂y∂F1​​dA∬D2​​∂x∂F2​​−∂y∂F1​​dA​=∮∂D1​​F⋅dr=∮∂D2​​F⋅dr.​

Notice also that ∂D1+∂D2=∂D=C1−C2\partial\mathscr{D}_1 + \partial\mathscr{D}_2 = \partial\mathscr{D} = \mathscr{C}_1 - \mathscr{C}_2∂D1​+∂D2​=∂D=C1​−C2​, so if we add everything up,

∬D∂F2∂x−∂F1∂y dA=∮C1F⃗⋅dr⃗−∮C2F⃗⋅dr⃗.\iint_{\mathscr{D}} \pderiv{F_2}{x} - \pderiv{F_1}{y} \,\diff{A} = \oint_{\mathscr{C}_1} \vec{F} \cdot \diff\vec{r} - \oint_{\mathscr{C}_2} \vec{F} \cdot \diff\vec{r}.∬D​∂x∂F2​​−∂y∂F1​​dA=∮C1​​F⋅dr−∮C2​​F⋅dr.

Plugging in the numbers we know, we get

∮∂C1F⃗⋅dr⃗=∬D∂F2∂x−∂F1∂y dA+∮∂C2F⃗⋅dr⃗=∬D−3 dA+12=−3⋅area⁡(D)+12=−3(60−4π)+12.\begin{aligned} \oint_{\partial\mathscr{C}_1} \vec{F} \cdot \diff\vec{r} &= \iint_{\mathscr{D}} \pderiv{F_2}{x} - \pderiv{F_1}{y} \,\diff{A} + \oint_{\partial\mathscr{C}_2} \vec{F} \cdot \diff\vec{r} \\ &= \iint_{\mathscr{D}} -3 \,\diff{A} + 12 \\ &= -3 \cdot \operatorname{area}\p{\mathscr{D}} + 12 \\ &= \boxed{-3\p{60 - 4\pi} + 12}. \end{aligned}∮∂C1​​F⋅dr​=∬D​∂x∂F2​​−∂y∂F1​​dA+∮∂C2​​F⋅dr=∬D​−3dA+12=−3⋅area(D)+12=−3(60−4π)+12​.​
