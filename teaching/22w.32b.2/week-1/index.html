Direct Integration

This "technique" is used when the integrand is the derivative of a function that you already know. Basically, if you remember the common derivatives, then you already know a lot of integrals.

Power Rule

The derivative of xnx^nxn is nxn−1nx^{n-1}nxn−1, so you immediately get

∫nxn−1 dx=xn+C  ⟹  ∫xn−1 dx=xnn+C.\int nx^{n-1} \,\diff{x} = x^n + C \implies \int x^{n-1} \,\diff{x} = \frac{x^n}{n} + C.∫nxn−1dx=xn+C⟹∫xn−1dx=nxn​+C.

(I absorbed the nnn into the CCC, since nnn is just a constant.)

Trig Functions

Recall the derivatives of the trig functions:

ddxsin⁡x=−cos⁡xddxcos⁡x=−sin⁡xddxtan⁡x=−sec⁡2xddxcot⁡x=−csc⁡2xddxsec⁡x=−sec⁡xtan⁡xddxcsc⁡x=−csc⁡xcot⁡x\begin{aligned} \deriv{}{x} \sin{x} &= \phantom{-}\cos{x} \\ \deriv{}{x} \cos{x} &= -\sin{x} \\ \deriv{}{x} \tan{x} &= \phantom{-}\sec^2{x} \\ \deriv{}{x} \cot{x} &= -\csc^2{x} \\ \deriv{}{x} \sec{x} &= \phantom{-}\sec{x}\tan{x} \\ \deriv{}{x} \csc{x} &= -\csc{x}\cot{x} \end{aligned}dxd​sinxdxd​cosxdxd​tanxdxd​cotxdxd​secxdxd​cscx​=−cosx=−sinx=−sec2x=−csc2x=−secxtanx=−cscxcotx​

This becomes a list of integrals:

∫cos⁡x dx=−sin⁡x+C∫sin⁡x dx=−cos⁡x+C∫sec⁡2x dx=−tan⁡x+C∫csc⁡2x dx=−cot⁡x+C∫sec⁡xtan⁡x dx=−sec⁡x+C∫csc⁡xcot⁡x dx=−csc⁡x+C\begin{aligned} \int \cos{x} \,\diff{x} &= \phantom{-}\sin{x} + C \\ \int \sin{x} \,\diff{x} &= -\cos{x} + C \\ \int \sec^2{x} \,\diff{x} &= \phantom{-}\tan{x} + C \\ \int \csc^2{x} \,\diff{x} &= -\cot{x} + C \\ \int \sec{x}\tan{x} \,\diff{x} &= \phantom{-}\sec{x} + C \\ \int \csc{x}\cot{x} \,\diff{x} &= -\csc{x} + C \end{aligned}∫cosxdx∫sinxdx∫sec2xdx∫csc2xdx∫secxtanxdx∫cscxcotxdx​=−sinx+C=−cosx+C=−tanx+C=−cotx+C=−secx+C=−cscx+C​

Beyond these, there are a few weirder ones that don't really fit under direct integration, but are still good to know off the top of your head:

∫tan⁡x dx=−ln⁡∣sec⁡x∣+C∫sec⁡x dx=−ln⁡∣sec⁡x+tan⁡x∣+C∫cot⁡x dx=−ln⁡∣csc⁡x∣+C∫csc⁡x dx=−ln⁡∣csc⁡x+cot⁡x∣+C\begin{aligned} \int \tan{x} \,\diff{x} &= \phantom{-}\ln{\abs{\sec{x}}} + C \\ \int \sec{x} \,\diff{x} &= \phantom{-}\ln{\abs{\sec{x} + \tan{x}}} + C \\ \int \cot{x} \,\diff{x} &= -\ln{\abs{\csc{x}}} + C \\ \int \csc{x} \,\diff{x} &= -\ln{\abs{\csc{x} + \cot{x}}} + C \end{aligned}∫tanxdx∫secxdx∫cotxdx∫cscxdx​=−ln∣secx∣+C=−ln∣secx+tanx∣+C=−ln∣cscx∣+C=−ln∣cscx+cotx∣+C​

You can derive the ones for tan⁡x\tan{x}tanx and cot⁡x\cot{x}cotx using uuu-substitution. The ones for sec⁡x\sec{x}secx and csc⁡x\csc{x}cscx use a trick, though.

Exponentials and Logarithms

Like before, here are the common derivatives:

ddxbx=bxln⁡bddxlog⁡bx=1xln⁡b\begin{aligned} \deriv{}{x} b^x &= b^x \ln{b} \\[2ex] \deriv{}{x} \log_b{x} &= \frac{1}{x\ln{b}} \end{aligned}dxd​bxdxd​logb​x​=bxlnb=xlnb1​​

The first one gives you the integral

∫bx dx=bxln⁡b+C.\int b^x \,\diff{x} = \frac{b^x}{\ln{b}} + C.∫bxdx=lnbbx​+C.

When b=eb = eb=e, the second one gives you

∫1x dx=ln⁡∣x∣+C.\int \frac{1}{x} \,\diff{x} = \ln{\abs{x}} + C.∫x1​dx=ln∣x∣+C.

The reason for the absolute value is because if x<0x < 0x<0, then

ddxln⁡(−x)=1−x⋅−1=1x\deriv{}{x} \ln\p{-x} = \frac{1}{-x} \cdot -1 = \frac{1}{x}dxd​ln(−x)=−x1​⋅−1=x1​

by the chain rule. So both ln⁡x\ln{x}lnx and ln⁡(−x)\ln\p{-x}ln(−x) are antiderivatives for 1x\frac{1}{x}x1​ depending on the sign of xxx.

Inverse Trig Functions

Here are the derivatives:

ddxarcsin⁡x=−11−x2ddxarctan⁡x=−11+x2ddxarcsec⁡x=−1∣x∣x2−1ddxarccos⁡x=−11−x2ddxarccot⁡x=−11+x2ddxarccsc⁡x=−1∣x∣x2−1\begin{aligned} \deriv{}{x} \arcsin{x} &= \phantom{-}\frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \\ \deriv{}{x} \arctan{x} &= \phantom{-}\frac{1}{1 + x^2} \\ \deriv{}{x} \arcsec{x} &= \phantom{-}\frac{1}{\abs{x}\sqrt{x^2 - 1}} \\ \deriv{}{x} \arccos{x} &= -\frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \\ \deriv{}{x} \arccot{x} &= -\frac{1}{1 + x^2} \\ \deriv{}{x} \arccsc{x} &= -\frac{1}{\abs{x}\sqrt{x^2 - 1}} \\ \end{aligned}dxd​arcsinxdxd​arctanxdxd​arcsecxdxd​arccosxdxd​arccotxdxd​arccscx​=−1−x2​1​=−1+x21​=−∣x∣x2−1​1​=−1−x2​1​=−1+x21​=−∣x∣x2−1​1​​

So you have these integrals:

∫11−x2 dx=arcsin⁡x+C∫11+x2 dx=arctan⁡x+C∫1∣x∣x2−1 dx=arcsec⁡x+C\begin{aligned} \int \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \,\diff{x} &= \arcsin{x} + C \\ \int \frac{1}{1 + x^2} \,\diff{x} &= \arctan{x} + C \\ \int \frac{1}{\abs{x}\sqrt{x^2 - 1}} \,\diff{x} &= \arcsec{x} + C \end{aligned}∫1−x2​1​dx∫1+x21​dx∫∣x∣x2−1​1​dx​=arcsinx+C=arctanx+C=arcsecx+C​

Symmetry

Sometimes, you need to calculate a definite integral, but you "can't" integrate something (i.e., you can't express the antiderivative in terms of trig functions, exponentials, polynomials, etc.). In some cases, you can still write down the answer:

Example 1.

Calculate ∫−22sin⁡(x3) dx\displaystyle \int_{-2}^2 \sin\p{x^3} \,\diff{x}∫−22​sin(x3)dx.
Solution.

You won't be able to integrate f(x)=sin⁡(x3)f\p{x} = \sin\p{x^3}f(x)=sin(x3) no matter how hard you try. However, this function has a special property-it's an odd function:

f(−x)=sin⁡((−x)3)=sin⁡(−x3)=−sin⁡(x3)=−f(x).f\p{-x} = \sin\p{\p{-x}^3} = \sin\p{-x^3} = -\sin\p{x^3} = -f\p{x}.f(−x)=sin((−x)3)=sin(−x3)=−sin(x3)=−f(x).

Moreover, the definite integral we're trying to calculate has symmetric bounds, so the integral of fff on [−2,0]\br{-2, 0}[−2,0] will cancel out with the integral on [0,2]\br{0, 2}[0,2]. In other words,

∫−22sin⁡(x3) dx=0.\int_{-2}^2 \sin\p{x^3} \,\diff{x} = \boxed{0}.∫−22​sin(x3)dx=0​.

uuu-substitution

uuu-substitution is the "reverse chain rule." This means you'll want to try it when you see a function and its derivative (maybe times a constant).

Example 2.

Calculate ∫xsin⁡(x2) dx\displaystyle \int x\sin\p{x^2} \,\diff{x}∫xsin(x2)dx.
Solution.

Here, you can see x2x^2x2 and basically its derivative 2x2x2x, except we're missing a 222. This means we'll want to try u=x2u = x^2u=x2, which gives du=2x dx\diff{u} = 2x \,\diff{x}du=2xdx, so the integral becomes

∫xsin⁡(x2) dx=∫sin⁡u du2=−12cos⁡u+C=−12cos⁡(x2)+C.\begin{aligned} \int x\sin\p{x^2} \,\diff{x} &= \int \sin{u} \,\frac{\diff{u}}{2} \\ &= -\frac{1}{2} \cos{u} + C \\ &= \boxed{-\frac{1}{2} \cos\p{x^2} + C}. \end{aligned}∫xsin(x2)dx​=∫sinu2du​=−21​cosu+C=−21​cos(x2)+C​.​
Example 3.

Calculate ∫dxxln⁡x\displaystyle \int \frac{\diff{x}}{x\ln{x}}∫xlnxdx​.
Solution.

We can write the integral as

∫1x⋅1ln⁡x dx,\int \frac{1}{x} \cdot \frac{1}{\ln{x}} \,\diff{x},∫x1​⋅lnx1​dx,

and from here, it's more clear that we see ln⁡x\ln{x}lnx and its derivative, so we can set u=ln⁡xu = \ln{x}u=lnx to get

∫dxxln⁡x=∫duu=ln⁡∣u∣+C=ln⁡∣ln⁡x∣+C.\begin{aligned} \int \frac{\diff{x}}{x\ln{x}} &= \int \frac{\diff{u}}{u} \\ &= \ln\abs{u} + C \\ &= \boxed{\ln\abs{\ln{x}} + C}. \end{aligned}∫xlnxdx​​=∫udu​=ln∣u∣+C=ln∣lnx∣+C​.​

Calculate ∫dxx(ln⁡x)ln⁡(ln⁡x)\displaystyle \int \frac{\diff{x}}{x\p{\ln{x}}\ln\p{\ln{x}}}∫x(lnx)ln(lnx)dx​.

Integration by Parts

Similar to uuu-substitution, integration by parts is a "reverse product rule."

Theorem (integration by parts)
∫u dv=uv−∫v du\int u \,\diff{v} = uv - \int v \,\diff{u}∫udv=uv−∫vdu

Equivalently, you can write this as

∫f(x)g′(x) dx=f(x)g(x)−∫f′(x)g(x) dx.\int f\p{x} g'\p{x} \,\diff{x} = f\p{x}g\p{x} - \int f'\p{x}g\p{x} \,\diff{x}.∫f(x)g′(x)dx=f(x)g(x)−∫f′(x)g(x)dx.

In other words, integration by parts lets you replace f(x)f\p{x}f(x) with its derivative f′(x)f'\p{x}f′(x), which can sometimes simplify the integral. For example, if f(x)=ln⁡xf\p{x} = \ln{x}f(x)=lnx, then f′(x)=1xf'\p{x} = \frac{1}{x}f′(x)=x1​, which is generally easier to work with when integrating.

There are two main scenarios where you want to use integration by parts:

  1. When you want to integrate a product of two "random" functions.
  2. When you want to integrate a "weird" function by itself.
Example 4.

Calculate ∫xcos⁡x dx\displaystyle \int x\cos{x} \,\diff{x}∫xcosxdx.
Solution.

Here, we're in scenario 1 above. For this problem, you will want to use the parts u=xu = xu=x and dv=cos⁡x dv\diff{v} = \cos{x} \,\diff{v}dv=cosxdv, which give

du=dxandv=sin⁡x.\diff{u} = \diff{x} \quad\text{and}\quad v = \sin{x}.du=dxandv=sinx.

Then

∫xcos⁡x dx=∫u dv=uv−∫v du=xsin⁡x−∫sin⁡x dx=xsin⁡x+cos⁡x+C.\begin{aligned} \int x\cos{x} \,\diff{x} &= \int u \,\diff{v} \\ &= uv - \int v \,\diff{u} \\ &= x\sin{x} - \int \sin{x} \,\diff{x} \\ &= \boxed{x\sin{x} + \cos{x} + C}. \end{aligned}∫xcosxdx​=∫udv=uv−∫vdu=xsinx−∫sinxdx=xsinx+cosx+C​.​
Example 5.

Calculate ∫arcsin⁡x dx\displaystyle \int \arcsin{x} \,\diff{x}∫arcsinxdx.
Solution.

This time, we're in scenario 2. When this happens, we can just let u=arcsin⁡xu = \arcsin{x}u=arcsinx and dv=dx\diff{v} = \diff{x}dv=dx, so

du=dx1−x2andv=x.\diff{u} = \frac{\diff{x}}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \quad\text{and}\quad v = x.du=1−x2​dx​andv=x.

Plugging everything in,

∫arcsin⁡x dx=∫u dv=uv−∫v du=xarcsin⁡x−∫x1−x2 dx.\begin{aligned} \int \arcsin{x} \,\diff{x} &= \int u \,\diff{v} \\ &= uv - \int v \,\diff{u} \\ &= x\arcsin{x} - \int \frac{x}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \,\diff{x}. \end{aligned}∫arcsinxdx​=∫udv=uv−∫vdu=xarcsinx−∫1−x2​x​dx.​

So, we just need to calculate this new integral, which can be done using the substitution w=1−x2w = 1 - x^2w=1−x2. This gives us dw=−2x dx\diff{w} = -2x \,\diff{x}dw=−2xdx, so

∫x1−x2 dx=−12∫dww=−w+C=−1−x2+C.\begin{aligned} \int \frac{x}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \,\diff{x} &= -\frac{1}{2} \int \frac{\diff{w}}{\sqrt{w}} \\ &= -\sqrt{w} + C \\ &= -\sqrt{1 - x^2} + C. \end{aligned}∫1−x2​x​dx​=−21​∫w​dw​=−w​+C=−1−x2​+C.​

Thus,

∫arcsin⁡x dx=xarcsin⁡x+1−x2+C.\int \arcsin{x} \,\diff{x} = \boxed{x\arcsin{x} + \sqrt{1 - x^2} + C}.∫arcsinxdx=xarcsinx+1−x2​+C​.

Trig Substitution

You usually want to try this technique when you see something similar to 1+x21 + x^21+x2 or 1−x21 - x^21−x2 and a uuu-substitution doesn't work. When this happens, you'll want to choose your substitution as follows:

1−x2⇝x=sin⁡θ1+x2⇝x=tan⁡θ\begin{aligned} 1 - x^2 &\rightsquigarrow x = \sin{\theta} \\ 1 + x^2 &\rightsquigarrow x = \tan{\theta} \end{aligned}1−x21+x2​⇝x=sinθ⇝x=tanθ​
Example 6.

Calculate ∫dx1+x2\displaystyle \int \frac{\diff{x}}{\sqrt{1 + x^2}}∫1+x2​dx​.
Solution.

Any uuu-substitution you try won't work here since there's no xxx in the numerator, so we'll have to try a trig substitution. We're in the 1+x21 + x^21+x2 case, so we can set x=tan⁡θx = \tan{\theta}x=tanθ, which means dx=sec⁡2θ dθ\diff{x} = \sec^2{\theta} \,\diff{\theta}dx=sec2θdθ. Then

∫dx1+x2=∫sec⁡2θ1+tan⁡2θ dθ=∫sec⁡2θsec⁡θ dθ=∫sec⁡θ dθ=ln⁡∣sec⁡θ+tan⁡θ∣+C.\begin{aligned} \int \frac{\diff{x}}{\sqrt{1 + x^2}} &= \int \frac{\sec^2{\theta}}{\sqrt{1 + \tan^2{\theta}}} \,\diff{\theta} \\ &= \int \frac{\sec^2{\theta}}{\sec{\theta}} \,\diff{\theta} \\ &= \int \sec{\theta} \,\diff{\theta} \\ &= \ln\abs{\sec{\theta} + \tan{\theta}} + C. \end{aligned}∫1+x2​dx​​=∫1+tan2θ​sec2θ​dθ=∫secθsec2θ​dθ=∫secθdθ=ln∣secθ+tanθ∣+C.​

To finish the problem, we need to undo the substitution. We already know that tan⁡θ=x\tan{\theta} = xtanθ=x (this was the substitution we started with), and for sec⁡θ\sec{\theta}secθ, we can use our trig identities:

sec⁡2θ=1+tan⁡2θ=1+x2  ⟹  sec⁡θ=1+x2.\sec^2{\theta} = 1 + \tan^2{\theta} = 1 + x^2 \implies \sec{\theta} = \sqrt{1 + x^2}.sec2θ=1+tan2θ=1+x2⟹secθ=1+x2​.

Substituting everything back in, we get

∫dx1+x2=ln⁡∣1+x2+x∣+C.\int \frac{\diff{x}}{\sqrt{1 + x^2}} = \boxed{\ln\abs{\sqrt{1 + x^2} + x} + C}.∫1+x2​dx​=ln∣∣​1+x2​+x∣∣​+C​.
Example 7.

Calculate ∫1−4x2 dx\displaystyle \int \sqrt{1 - 4x^2} \,\diff{x}∫1−4x2​dx.
Solution.

If you don't remember your trig identities (like the double-angle identities), then it may be helpful to take a look at this review sheet I made for my 31B students last quarter (you can ignore everything starting at Series).

For this problem, we're essentially in the 1−x21 - x^21−x2 case, though it's not exactly in that form yet. We can write

1−4x2=1−(2x)2,\sqrt{1 - 4x^2} = \sqrt{1 - \p{2x}^2},1−4x2​=1−(2x)2​,

so replacing xxx with 2x2x2x, we get the substitution 2x=sin⁡θ2x = \sin{\theta}2x=sinθ. Then 2 dx=cos⁡θ dθ2 \,\diff{x} = \cos{\theta} \,\diff{\theta}2dx=cosθdθ, and we get

∫1−4x2 dx=12∫1−sin⁡2θcos⁡θ dθ=12∫cos⁡2θ dθ=12∫1+cos⁡2θ2 dθ(double-angle identity)=14∫1+cos⁡2θ dθ=14(θ+12sin⁡2θ)+C=14θ+18sin⁡2θ+C.\begin{aligned} \int \sqrt{1 - 4x^2} \,\diff{x} &= \frac{1}{2} \int \sqrt{1 - \sin^2{\theta}} \cos{\theta} \,\diff{\theta} \\ &= \frac{1}{2} \int \cos^2{\theta} \,\diff{\theta} \\ &= \frac{1}{2} \int \frac{1 + \cos{2\theta}}{2} \,\diff{\theta} && \p{\text{double-angle identity}} \\ &= \frac{1}{4} \int 1 + \cos{2\theta} \,\diff{\theta} \\ &= \frac{1}{4} \p{\theta + \frac{1}{2} \sin{2\theta}} + C \\ &= \frac{1}{4} \theta + \frac{1}{8} \sin{2\theta} + C. \end{aligned}∫1−4x2​dx​=21​∫1−sin2θ​cosθdθ=21​∫cos2θdθ=21​∫21+cos2θ​dθ=41​∫1+cos2θdθ=41​(θ+21​sin2θ)+C=41​θ+81​sin2θ+C.​(double-angle identity)

Solving for θ\thetaθ from 2x=sin⁡θ2x = \sin{\theta}2x=sinθ, we get θ=arcsin⁡2x\theta = \arcsin{2x}θ=arcsin2x. To undo the substitution for the other term, we'll need another identity:

sin⁡2θ=2sin⁡θcos⁡θ\sin{2\theta} = 2\sin{\theta}\cos{\theta}sin2θ=2sinθcosθ

We already know sin⁡θ=2x\sin{\theta} = 2xsinθ=2x, and to get cos⁡θ\cos{\theta}cosθ,

cos⁡θ=1−sin⁡2θ=1−4x2,\cos{\theta} = \sqrt{1 - \sin^2{\theta}} = \sqrt{1 - 4x^2},cosθ=1−sin2θ​=1−4x2​,

which means

sin⁡2θ=2⋅2x⋅1−4x2=4x1−4x2.\sin{2\theta} = 2 \cdot 2x \cdot \sqrt{1 - 4x^2} = 4x\sqrt{1 - 4x^2}.sin2θ=2⋅2x⋅1−4x2​=4x1−4x2​.

Putting everything together, we end up with

∫1−4x2 dx=14arcsin⁡2x+12x1−4x2+C.\int \sqrt{1 - 4x^2} \,\diff{x} = \boxed{\frac{1}{4} \arcsin{2x} + \frac{1}{2} x\sqrt{1 - 4x^2} + C}.∫1−4x2​dx=41​arcsin2x+21​x1−4x2​+C​.
