Lagrange Multipliers

The setup is this: we want to optimize a function f(x,y)f\p{x, y}f(x,y) subject to some constraint g(x,y)=cg\p{x, y} = cg(x,y)=c. In other words, we want to maximize and minimize f(x,y)f\p{x, y}f(x,y), where (x,y)\p{x, y}(x,y) lives on a level curve.

With this setup, the critical points are the points that satisfy the Lagrange multiplier equation

∇f(x,y)=λ∇g(x,y)\nabla{f}\p{x, y} = \lambda \nabla{g}\p{x, y}∇f(x,y)=λ∇g(x,y)

or points where ∇f,∇g\nabla f, \nabla g∇f,∇g don't exist.

There is a technicality with this, though. If there are a global max and global min, then they have to satisfy the equation. But sometimes, you don't have a global max or a global min, so you need to have an idea of what extrema there are.

Bounded Constraints

If your constraint is bounded, then there will always be both a global min and a global max.

Unbounded Constraints

If your constraint is unbounded, then there's a chance that you don't have a global min or max. In this case, you'll have to figure out if you have any based on what fff and ggg are.

Example 1.

Optimize f(x,y)=x2+y2f\p{x, y} = x^2 + y^2f(x,y)=x2+y2 subject to 2x+3y=62x + 3y = 62x+3y=6.
Solution.

For this problem, the constraint is unbounded because it's a line. If xxx and yyy are large (either large positive numbers or large negative numbers), then f(x,y)f\p{x, y}f(x,y) will also become very large. This tells you that fff doesn't have a maximum. You can prove it like this: you can solve the constraint for xxx to get

x=3−32y.x = 3 - \frac{3}{2}y.x=3−23​y.

Plugging this into f(x,y)f\p{x, y}f(x,y), you get

f(3−32y,y)=(3−32y)2+y2.\begin{aligned} f\p{3 - \frac{3}{2}y, y} &= \p{3 - \frac{3}{2}y}^2 + y^2. \end{aligned}f(3−23​y,y)​=(3−23​y)2+y2.​

Then

lim⁡y→∞f(3−32y,y)=lim⁡y→−∞f(3−32y,y)=∞.\lim_{y\to\infty} f\p{3 - \frac{3}{2}y, y} = \lim_{y\to-\infty} f\p{3 - \frac{3}{2}y, y} = \infty.y→∞lim​f(3−23​y,y)=y→−∞lim​f(3−23​y,y)=∞.

In this situation, you still get a global minimum, so we can try the Lagrange multiplier equation. The gradients are

∇f(x,y)=⟨2x,2y⟩∇g(x,y)=⟨2,3⟩,\begin{aligned} \nabla{f}\p{x, y} &= \ang{2x, 2y} \\ \nabla{g}\p{x, y} &= \ang{2, 3}, \end{aligned}∇f(x,y)∇g(x,y)​=⟨2x,2y⟩=⟨2,3⟩,​

so the equation is

⟨2x,2y⟩=λ⟨2,3⟩.\ang{2x, 2y} = \lambda\ang{2, 3}.⟨2x,2y⟩=λ⟨2,3⟩.

The first coordinate gives us x=λx = \lambdax=λ, and the second coordinate gives us y=3λ2y = \frac{3\lambda}{2}y=23λ​. Plugging this into the constraint,

2λ+3(3λ2)=6  ⟹  λ=1213.2\lambda + 3\p{\frac{3\lambda}{2}} = 6 \implies \lambda = \frac{12}{13}.2λ+3(23λ​)=6⟹λ=1312​.

Thus, (x,y)=(1213,1813)\p{x, y} = \p{\frac{12}{13}, \frac{18}{13}}(x,y)=(1312​,1318​) is our critical point. Since there's a global minimum, it must be a critical point, and since there's only one critical point, this means that the minimum is

f(1213,1813)=3613.f\p{\frac{12}{13}, \frac{18}{13}} = \frac{36}{13}.f(1312​,1318​)=1336​.
