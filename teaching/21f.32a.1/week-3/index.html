Planes

When specifying a line, we just need two pieces of information: a point r⃗0\vec{r}_0r0​ on the line and the direction v⃗\vec{v}v that the line points in. Then any point on the line can be represented by r⃗(t)=r⃗0+tv⃗\vec{r}\p{t} = \vec{r}_0 + t\vec{v}r(t)=r0​+tv.

Similar to a line, to specify a plane, we still need a point r⃗0\vec{r}_0r0​ on the plane. However, a plane doesn't really have a direction, but it does have a tilt. If you think about it, a normal vector n⃗\vec{n}n to the plane is a succinct way of describing tilt. Then given any point r⃗\vec{r}r on the line, the vector r⃗−r⃗0\vec{r} - \vec{r}_0r−r0​ is parallel to the plane, so we get

(r⃗−r⃗0)⋅n⃗=0.\p{\vec{r} - \vec{r}_0} \cdot \vec{n} = 0.(r−r0​)⋅n=0.

If we let r⃗=⟨x,y,z⟩\vec{r} = \ang{x, y, z}r=⟨x,y,z⟩, r⃗0=⟨x0,y0,z0⟩\vec{r}_0 = \ang{x_0, y_0, z_0}r0​=⟨x0​,y0​,z0​⟩, and n⃗=⟨a,b,c⟩\vec{n} = \ang{a, b, c}n=⟨a,b,c⟩, then the equation becomes

⟨x−x0,y−y0,z−z0⟩⋅⟨a,b,c⟩=0ax−ax0+by−by0+cz−cz0=0ax+by+cz=ax0+by0+cz0⏟= dax+by+cz=d.\begin{gathered} \ang{x - x_0, y - y_0, z - z_0} \cdot \ang{a, b, c} = 0 \\ ax - ax_0 + by - by_0 + cz - cz_0 = 0 \\ ax + by + cz = \underbrace{ax_0 + by_0 + cz_0}_{=\,d} \\ ax + by + cz = d. \end{gathered}⟨x−x0​,y−y0​,z−z0​⟩⋅⟨a,b,c⟩=0ax−ax0​+by−by0​+cz−cz0​=0ax+by+cz==dax0​+by0​+cz0​​​ax+by+cz=d.​

Note that the coefficients of our normal vector n⃗\vec{n}n are the coefficients on x,y,zx, y, zx,y,z. This means that given the equation of a plane, you can immediately write down a normal vector just by looking at the coefficients.

Scalar Triple Product

The scalar triple product u⃗⋅(v⃗×w⃗)\vec{u} \cdot \p{\vec{v} \times \vec{w}}u⋅(v×w) is the (signed) volume of the parallelepiped spanned by u⃗,v⃗,w⃗\vec{u}, \vec{v}, \vec{w}u,v,w. This means that if the scalar triple product is 000, then the parallelepiped is flat, i.e., u⃗,v⃗,w⃗\vec{u}, \vec{v}, \vec{w}u,v,w must be coplanar.

Collision vs. Intersection

If r⃗1(t)\vec{r}_1\p{t}r1​(t) and r⃗2(t)\vec{r}_2\p{t}r2​(t) are two curves, then:

  1. They intersect when r⃗1(t)=r⃗2(s)\vec{r}_1\p{t} = \vec{r}_2\p{s}r1​(t)=r2​(s) (note the different variables).
  2. They collide when r⃗1(t)=r⃗2(t)\vec{r}_1\p{t} = \vec{r}_2\p{t}r1​(t)=r2​(t) (note the variables are the same).
