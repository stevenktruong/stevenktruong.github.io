Uniform Convergence

There's not too much to say about this section in the book since a lot of my examples have been related to this concept, and most of the properties about uniform convergence of functions fn ⁣:R→R\func{f_n}{\R}{\R}fn​:R→R hold for more general functions fn ⁣:(X,dX)→(Y,dY)\func{f_n}{\p{X, d_X}}{\p{Y, d_Y}}fn​:(X,dX​)→(Y,dY​). For example, if {fn}n\set{f_n}_n{fn​}n​ is a sequence of continuous functions that converges uniformly to a function fff, then fff is also continuous.

A lot of the examples we did were on the space C([0,1])C\p{\br{0, 1}}C([0,1]), i.e., the metric space of continuous functions on [0,1]\br{0, 1}[0,1] with the sup⁡\supsup-metric. However, the general definitions are slightly different:

Definition

Let (X,dX)\p{X, d_X}(X,dX​) and (Y,dY)\p{Y, d_Y}(Y,dY​) be metric spaces. We define

B(X,Y)={f ⁣:X→Y | f is bounded}Cb(X,Y)={f ⁣:X→Y | f is bounded and continuous}.\begin{aligned} B\p{X, Y} &= \set{\func{f}{X}{Y} \st f \text{ is bounded}} \\ C_b\p{X, Y} &= \set{\func{f}{X}{Y} \st f \text{ is bounded and continuous}}. \end{aligned}B(X,Y)Cb​(X,Y)​={f:X→Y∣f is bounded}={f:X→Y∣f is bounded and continuous}.​

When equipped with the sup⁡\supsup-metric, i.e.,

d(f,g)=sup⁡x∈X∣f(x)−g(x)∣,d\p{f, g} = \sup_{x \in X}{\abs{f\p{x} - g\p{x}}},d(f,g)=x∈Xsup​∣f(x)−g(x)∣,

these function spaces are metric spaces.
Remark.

The key difference here is the additional assumption that our functions are bounded. In our example C([0,1])C\p{\br{0, 1}}C([0,1]), we're using X=[0,1]X = \br{0, 1}X=[0,1], which is compact. Thus, continuous functions are automatically bounded, so we didn't need to include it in the definition.

Boundedness is important for our metric to make sense. For example, if X=Y=RX = Y = \RX=Y=R and f(x)=x2f\p{x} = x^2f(x)=x2, then

d(f,0)=sup⁡x∈R∣x2−0∣=∞.d\p{f, 0} = \sup_{x \in \R}{\abs{x^2 - 0}} = \infty.d(f,0)=x∈Rsup​∣∣​x2−0∣∣​=∞.

The main result is the following theorem:

Theorem

Let (X,dX)\p{X, d_X}(X,dX​) be a metric space, and let (Y,dY)\p{Y, d_Y}(Y,dY​) be a complete metric space. Then B(X,Y)B\p{X, Y}B(X,Y) and Cb(X,Y)C_b\p{X, Y}Cb​(X,Y) are complete.
Remark.

Note that it's important that YYY is a complete metric space. In the proof, we use it to find a pointwise limit of a Cauchy (with respect to the sup⁡\supsup-metric) sequence.

Dini's Theorem

In general, uniform convergence implies pointwise convergence, but the converse is not true, i.e., there are sequences which converge pointwise but not uniformly. In some very special cases, though, we can upgrade pointwise convergence to uniform convergence:

Theorem (Dini)

Let (X,dX)\p{X, d_X}(X,dX​) and (Y,dY)\p{Y, d_Y}(Y,dY​) be metric spaces, and let fn,f ⁣:X→R\func{f_n, f}{X}{\R}fn​,f:X→R be functions. Assume that:

  1. XXX is compact
  2. f,fnf, f_nf,fn​ are continuous for all nnn

Then fn→ff_n \to ffn​→f uniformly.

Example 5.

True or false: If fnf_nfn​ is a sequence of differentiable functions on [−1,1]\br{-1, 1}[−1,1] and fn→ff_n \to ffn​→f uniformly, then fff is differentiable at at least one point.
Solution.

This is also false. The Weierstrass function is the uniform limit of differentiable functions (in fact, these functions are differentiable infinitely many times), but is differentiable nowhere.
