Uniform Convergence and Differentiability

In Week 7, we saw that even if fn→ff_n \to ffn​→f uniformly, fff is not guaranteed to be differentiability at even a single point. This tells us that uniform convergence of fnf_nfn​ is independent from the differentiability of fff, so our conditions will need to look relatively different:

Theorem

Assume that fn,g ⁣:[a,b]→R\func{f_n,g}{\br{a,b}}{\R}fn​,g:[a,b]→R are functions such that:

  1. fn′f_n'fn′​ exists for all n∈Nn \in \Nn∈N
  2. fn′→gf_n' \to gfn′​→g uniformly on [a,b]\br{a, b}[a,b]
  3. there exists x0∈[a,b]x_0 \in \br{a, b}x0​∈[a,b] such that {fn(x0)}n\set{f_n\p{x_0}}_n{fn​(x0​)}n​ converges

Then fnf_nfn​ converges uniformly to a function fff on [a,b]\br{a, b}[a,b] and fff is differentiable with f′=gf' = gf′=g on (a,b)\p{a, b}(a,b).
Remark.

The theorem tells us that under the conditions above, we can swap limits in the following:

lim⁡n→∞lim⁡h→0fn(x+h)−fn(x)h=lim⁡h→0lim⁡n→∞fn(x+h)−fn(x)h\lim_{n\to\infty} \lim_{h\to0} \frac{f_n\p{x+h} - f_n\p{x}}{h} = \lim_{h\to0} \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{f_n\p{x+h} - f_n\p{x}}{h}n→∞lim​h→0lim​hfn​(x+h)−fn​(x)​=h→0lim​n→∞lim​hfn​(x+h)−fn​(x)​

The proof of the theorem is quite hard, which tells you that swapping limits is hard in general, so always take a second to think before you try swapping things!

This theorem is very useful when dealing with power series. For now, I'll illustrate its usefulness with a concrete example:

Example 1.

Notice that the formula for f′f'f′ says

ddx∑n=0∞xn=∑n=0∞ddxxn.\deriv{}{x} \sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \deriv{}{x} x^n.dxd​n=0∑∞​xn=n=0∑∞​dxd​xn.

To take a derivative, we need to take a limit, and to calculate an infinite sum, we need to take another limit. This tells us that even though it looks obvious, this fact is not trivial since we need to interchange two limits.

To show that fff is differentiable on (−1,1)\p{-1, 1}(−1,1), we can just show that fff is differentiable on any strict subset (−r,r)\p{-r, r}(−r,r), where 0≤r<10 \leq r < 10≤r<1. We want to restrict ourselves to a smaller set for two reasons:

  1. Differentiability is a local property (i.e., f′(x)f'\p{x}f′(x) only depends on values of fff really close to xxx), meaning we don't have to use the whole set to prove things about it.
  2. Bad things happen close to the endpoints −1-1−1 and 111, so we want to avoid them.

Let fN(x)=∑n=0Nxnf_N\p{x} = \sum_{n=0}^N x^nfN​(x)=∑n=0N​xn. We'll try to apply the theorem above to this sequence.

Because fNf_NfN​ is a finite sum, we already know

Example 2.

True or False

Example 3.

True or false: If fn→ff_n \to ffn​→f uniformly on compact sets of R\RR, then fn→ff_n \to ffn​→f uniformly on all of R\RR.
Solution.

This is false. A simple example is a moving step function, i.e.,

sup⁡x∈R∣fn(x)−0∣=1,\sup_{x \in \R}{\abs{f_n\p{x} - 0}} = 1,x∈Rsup​∣fn​(x)−0∣=1,

so fnf_nfn​ does not converge uniformly to 000 on all of R\RR.
