Connectedness

Definition

Let (X,d)\p{X, d}(X,d) be a metric space. We say that XXX is disconnected if there exist two disjoint, non-empty, open sets U,V⊆XU, V \subseteq XU,V⊆X such that X=U∪VX = U \cup VX=U∪V.

If XXX is not disconnected, then we say that XXX is connected.
Example 1.

  • Any interval in R\RR is connected (with respect to the restricted absolute value metric).

  • Q⊆R\Q \subseteq \RQ⊆R is disconnected (with respect to the restricted absolute value metric).
Example 2.
(warmup to 2.4.6)

Let (X,d)\p{X, d}(X,d) be a metric space and assume A,BA, BA,B are connected sets in XXX such that A∩B=∅A \cap B = \emptysetA∩B=∅. Show that A∪BA \cup BA∪B is connected.
Solution.

Assume that A∪B=U∪VA \cup B = U \cup VA∪B=U∪V, where U,VU, VU,V are disjoint, non-empty, open sets in A∪BA \cup BA∪B. By the properties of sub-metric spaces, there exist open sets UX,VX⊆XU_X, V_X \subseteq XUX​,VX​⊆X in XXX such that U=(A∪B)∩UXU = \p{A \cup B} \cap U_XU=(A∪B)∩UX​ and V=(A∪B)∩VXV = \p{A \cup B} \cap V_XV=(A∪B)∩VX​.

Claim: A⊆UA \subseteq UA⊆U or A⊆VA \subseteq VA⊆V.

Suppose otherwise, and that A∩U≠∅A \cap U \neq \emptysetA∩U=∅ and A∩V≠∅A \cap V \neq \emptysetA∩V=∅. Notice that

A∩U=A∩(A∪B)∩UX=A∩UX,A \cap U = A \cap \p{A \cup B} \cap U_X = A \cap U_X,A∩U=A∩(A∪B)∩UX​=A∩UX​,

i.e., A∩UA \cap UA∩U is open in AAA and by symmetry, A∩VA \cap VA∩V is open in AAA also. But A∪B=U∪VA \cup B = U \cup VA∪B=U∪V, so

A=A∩(A∪B)=A∩(U∪V)(since A∪B=U∪V)=(A∩U)∪(A∩V).\begin{aligned} A &= A \cap \p{A \cup B} \\ &= A \cap \p{U \cup V} && \p{\text{since }A \cup B = U \cup V} \\ &= \p{A \cap U} \cup \p{A \cap V}. \end{aligned}A​=A∩(A∪B)=A∩(U∪V)=(A∩U)∪(A∩V).​​(since A∪B=U∪V)​

But A∩UA \cap UA∩U and A∩VA \cap VA∩V are disjoint, non-empty, and open sets in AAA, which implies that AAA is disconnected, a contradiction.

Thus, A⊆UA \subseteq UA⊆U or A⊆VA \subseteq VA⊆V. Without loss of generality, we may assume that A⊆UA \subseteq UA⊆U. In the other case, we can just replace UUU with VVV in the following argument.

Because A∩B≠∅A \cap B \neq \emptysetA∩B=∅, this means that B∩U≠∅B \cap U \neq \emptysetB∩U=∅. Since BBB is connected, this means that B⊆UB \subseteq UB⊆U as well by using the same argument in the claim, which means that A∪B⊆UA \cup B \subseteq UA∪B⊆U, so V=∅V = \emptysetV=∅, a contradiction. Thus, no such UUU and VVV existed to begin with, i.e., A∪BA \cup BA∪B is connected.
Example 3.

Let (X,d)\p{X, d}(X,d) be a connected metric space and let f ⁣:(X,dX)→(Y,dY)\func{f}{\p{X, d_X}}{\p{Y, d_Y}}f:(X,dX​)→(Y,dY​) be a function such that the set

Path-Connectedness

Definition

Let (X,d)\p{X, d}(X,d) be a metric space. Then XXX is path-connected if for any two points x,y∈Xx, y \in Xx,y∈X, there exists a continuous function γ ⁣:[0,1]→X\func{\gamma}{\br{0,1}}{X}γ:[0,1]→X such that γ(0)=x\gamma\p{0} = xγ(0)=x and γ(1)=y\gamma\p{1} = yγ(1)=y.
Remark.

In your homework, you will prove that path-connected   ⟹  \implies⟹ connected. However, the converse is not true in general, i.e., there are connected metric spaces which are not path-connected.

Sometimes, it's easier to show that a metric space is path-connected than it is to show that it's connected directly.

Example 4.

E={f∈C([0,1]) | f(0)=0}E = \set{f \in C\p{\br{0,1}} \st f\p{0} = 0}E={f∈C([0,1])∣f(0)=0}

Connectedness and Continuity

Proposition

If f ⁣:(X,dX)→(Y,dY)\func{f}{\p{X, d_X}}{\p{Y, d_Y}}f:(X,dX​)→(Y,dY​) is continuous and E⊆XE \subseteq XE⊆X is connected, then f(E)⊆Yf\p{E} \subseteq Yf(E)⊆Y is connected.

Like with compact sets, we say "the continuous image of a connected set is connected."

This can be a useful way to show that some sets are connected directly.

Example 5.

Let f ⁣:R→R\func{f}{\R}{\R}f:R→R be a continuous function. Show that its graph

Γ={(x,f(x)) | x∈R}\Gamma = \set{\p{x, f\p{x}} \st x \in \R}Γ={(x,f(x))∣x∈R}
