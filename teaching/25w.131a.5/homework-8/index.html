Common Mistakes

The most common mistake I saw was trying to do something like this to try to show that lim⁡x→aε(x)=0\lim_{x\to a} \varepsilon\p{x} = 0limx→a​ε(x)=0:

f′(a)=lim⁡x→a[f(x)−f(a)x−a]=lim⁡x→a[f′(a)−ε(x)]=f′(a)−lim⁡x→aε(x).f'\p{a} = \lim_{x\to a} \br{\frac{f\p{x} - f\p{a}}{x - a}} = \lim_{x\to a} \br{f'\p{a} - \varepsilon\p{x}} = f'\p{a} - \lim_{x\to a} \varepsilon\p{x}.f′(a)=x→alim​[x−af(x)−f(a)​]=x→alim​[f′(a)−ε(x)]=f′(a)−x→alim​ε(x).

Thus, solving gives lim⁡x→aε(x)=0\lim_{x\to a} \varepsilon\p{x} = 0limx→a​ε(x)=0.

The issue with this is that the argument only says this: if lim⁡x→aε(x)\lim_{x\to a} \varepsilon\p{x}limx→a​ε(x) exists, then it must be 000. However, you need to show that lim⁡x→aε(x)\lim_{x\to a} \varepsilon\p{x}limx→a​ε(x) exists to begin with. In other words, you can't just take limits on both sides and solve. The easiest way to do this is to use limit laws, which tells you that the limit exists and tells you have to compute the limit--you kill two birds with one stone.

This argument can be fixed, though it requires a little bit of work. See the following exercise.
